Safari 1.0

Version 1.0 · 2003
January 2003 · Mac OS X (Aqua)

Apple's first browser was built on a tiny open-source project almost nobody had heard of — and it ended up powering half the web.

A short history

Before Safari, the default browser on the Mac was Internet Explorer. In January 2003, Steve Jobs unveiled Safari with its signature brushed-metal window and blistering speed. Rather than build an engine from scratch, Apple forked KHTML — the rendering engine from KDE's little-known Konqueror browser on Linux — and turned it into WebKit.

That bet paid off beyond anyone's expectations: WebKit went on to power the iPhone, the iPad, and — until 2013 — Google Chrome itself.

Lesser-known & curious

  • An open-source surprise. Choosing KHTML startled even its KDE authors; merging Apple's enormous changes back upstream was, at first, painful enough to strain the relationship.
  • Chrome is a cousin. Google built Chrome on WebKit, then forked it into "Blink" in 2013 — but the family tree starts here.
  • The project's internal codename was "Alexander" — as in, conquer the world.
  • SnapBack let you jump straight back to your last typed address or search results — a beloved feature people still miss.
  • Safari was among the first browsers to ship a built-in pop-up blocker and private browsing.
  • The brushed-metal look was divisive even inside Apple, and was later quietly retired.
