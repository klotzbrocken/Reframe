Camino 2.0

2009 · Mac OS X
The Web browser that felt like it was made for the Mac

Mozilla’s power under the hood, Apple’s Aqua on the surface — Camino was Gecko wrapped in native Cocoa, built by and for Mac users.

A short history

Camino began in 2002 as Chimera, an experiment to pair Mozilla’s Gecko rendering engine with a genuine Cocoa interface instead of the cross-platform toolkit Mozilla used elsewhere. Renamed Camino in 2003, it became the Mac community’s answer to a browser that looked and behaved like a real Mac application — Aqua title bar, traffic-light controls, Keychain and Address Book integration, and the system-wide services Mac users expected.

Camino 2.0, released in November 2009, was the high-water mark: tab overview, a Top-Sites-style history page, built-in ad and Flash blocking, and full Growl support. As Firefox’s Mac build matured and Gecko moved on, the volunteer project wound down; Camino was formally retired in 2013.

Lesser-known & curious

  • The compass mark. Camino’s icon — a blue compass rose — nodded both to navigation and to the “trail” the name evokes (Spanish for “path” or “way”).
  • Cocoa, not XUL. Where Firefox drew its own widgets, Camino used Apple’s native controls, so its buttons, sheets and menus were the real system ones.
  • All-volunteer. Camino was never a paid Mozilla product; it was maintained by a small, dedicated community of Mac developers.
  • Ad-blocking built in. Long before it was fashionable, Camino shipped with optional ad and pop-up blocking baked right into the preferences.
  • PowerPC to Intel. Camino rode the whole transition — universal binaries that ran native on both the old G4/G5 Macs and the new Intel ones.
