Internet Explorer 6.0

Version 6.0 · 2001
August 2001 · Windows XP (Luna)

The browser that won the war — and then, for five long years, stopped fighting.

A short history

Internet Explorer 6 shipped alongside Windows XP in August 2001, dressed in the new blue-and-beige “Luna” look recreated by this theme. At its peak it held well over 90% of the market — the most dominant browser in history. With no serious rival left, Microsoft disbanded the IE team, and IE6 sat frozen while the web moved on around it.

That standstill made it infamous. Years of security holes, its own quirky box model and a stubborn refusal to support web standards turned IE6 into the browser developers loved to hate — eventually inspiring public “IE6 must die” campaigns and even a mock funeral in 2010.

Lesser-known & curious

  • The Luna toolbar you see here — full-colour icons, the green Back button and the fluttering Windows-flag throbber — were all new in the XP era and are cropped from a real IE6 SP3 screenshot.
  • SP3 never officially existed. IE6 got Service Packs 1 and 2 (the latter added the pop-up blocker and Information Bar); the “SP3” label came from being bundled into Windows XP SP3 in 2008.
  • It outlived three successors. IE7, 8 and 9 all shipped while corporate intranets still demanded IE6 — Microsoft finally ran a countdown site cheering as its share fell below 1%.
  • Conditional comments & the box model. A whole generation of CSS hacks (* html, the “double-margin” bug, <!--[if IE 6]>) exist purely because of this browser.
  • The Windows Messenger button on the toolbar launched the built-in chat client — kept here for authenticity even though it no longer rings anyone.
  • South Korea kept IE6 alive for over a decade because online banking laws were built around its ActiveX controls.
