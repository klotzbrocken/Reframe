Netscape Navigator 3.04 Gold

1997 · Windows 95
The browser that ran the early Web

The last of the Navigator 3 line — and, for millions, the browser the Web was born on.

A short history

Netscape Navigator grew out of NCSA Mosaic: in 1994 Marc Andreessen and Jim Clark founded Netscape Communications, and Navigator quickly became the most popular browser on Earth, defining what the Web looked and felt like. Version 3.0 shipped in 1996; 3.04 was the final maintenance release of that generation.

The “Gold” edition bundled a built-in WYSIWYG page editor — the “Edit” button on this toolbar — so you could author HTML in the same app you browsed with. Navigator introduced cookies, JavaScript, frames and the <blink> tag, and its throbbing “N” logo (recreated here) told a generation their page was loading.

Lesser-known & curious

  • Mozilla was the “Mosaic killer.” Netscape’s internal codename and dinosaur mascot lived on: when the source was opened in 1998 it became the Mozilla project — and eventually Firefox.
  • No address bar — a “Location” bar. Netscape labelled the URL field “Location:” with the globe icon beside it, exactly as recreated in this theme.
  • The Directory buttons. “What’s New?”, “What’s Cool?”, “Net Search” and friends were Netscape’s curated on-ramps to the young Web, hard-wired into the chrome.
  • Java, right there. The About screen proudly noted the bundled Java runtime from Sun — applets were the future, or so everyone thought.
  • The browser wars. 3.x fought Internet Explorer 3 head-to-head; within a couple of years IE, bundled with Windows, would overtake it.
  • AOL acquired Netscape in 1998; the Navigator brand was eventually retired, but its DNA runs through every modern browser.
