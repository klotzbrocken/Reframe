AOL Desktop 4.0

Version 4.0 · 1998 · Keyword: Welcome
1998 · Macintosh · Mac OS 9 · Dial-up

For millions of people in the late 1990s, AOL was the internet — and version 4.0 was the front door.

A short history

Released in 1998, America Online 4.0 was the most popular way to get online in the English-speaking world. It wrapped e-mail, chat, instant messaging, news, shopping and a curated web behind one friendly window — a walled garden you entered with a screen name and a modem's shriek. You didn't type URLs; you typed Keywords.

Its screech-and-handshake dial-up sound, the cheerful "You've Got Mail!", the running-man logo and the ever-present Buddy List defined a whole era of getting online. At its peak AOL carried a large share of all U.S. consumer internet traffic — and mailed out so many free trial CD-ROMs that they became a cultural artifact of their own.

Lesser-known & curious

  • "You've Got Mail" became a movie. The 1998 Tom Hanks / Meg Ryan film took its name — and its plot device — straight from the AOL greeting.
  • Everything was a window inside a window. AOL used an MDI shell: the Welcome screen, Channels, Mail and Buddy List all floated as child windows inside the one AOL frame.
  • AOL kept paying to bundle Internet Explorer even though it owned Netscape — the browser inside AOL was Microsoft's engine.
  • The famous voice — "Welcome!" and "You've Got Mail!" — was recorded by Elwood Edwards in 1989 for his wife, an early AOL employee.
  • Those CD-ROMs never stopped. AOL is estimated to have produced well over a billion trial discs; at one point a large fraction of all CDs manufactured on Earth were AOL discs.
  • AIM grew out of it. AOL Instant Messenger and the Buddy List introduced a generation to away-messages, screen names and the door-open/close sounds.
