The comeback — tabs, a search box and RSS, five years after Microsoft declared the browser war won.

A short history

Internet Explorer 7 arrived in October 2006, the first major IE release since IE6 in 2001. It shipped with Windows Vista and as a free update for Windows XP, wearing the light “Aero-lite” chrome this theme recreates — the round glass Back/Forward pearls, a full-width address bar, a dedicated Live-Search box and, at last, tabbed browsing.

It was as much a security project as a feature release: a new anti-phishing filter, a locked-down “Protected Mode” on Vista, and the removal of many legacy behaviours. After years of stagnation, IE7 was Microsoft answering Firefox — which had reached the tabs, extensions and standards support IE had ignored.

Lesser-known & curious

  • The menu bar vanished. IE7 was the first Explorer to hide File/Edit/View by default, moving everything into the Command Bar on the right of the tab row — recreated here with the original Home, Feeds, Print, Page and Tools glyphs.
  • RSS grew up. The orange Feeds button and the shared “Common Feed List” IE7 introduced became the little orange icon that appeared in half the web’s address bars for the next decade.
  • Quick Tabs & the search box. A thumbnail overview of every open tab, and a separate search field wired to a provider of your choice — MSN Search by default, soon renamed Live Search.
